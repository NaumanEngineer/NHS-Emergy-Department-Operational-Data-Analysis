Executive Summary

This report presents findings from a comprehensive exploratory data analysis of 2,000 ED episodes spanning 2023.

Through univariate analysis, normality testing, outlier detection, and bivariate correlation analysis, we identified three interconnected operational drivers of ED performance:

  • Staffing Adequacy (ρ = -0.85): Primary lever for reducing assessment delays. Each additional staff member reduces assessment wait by ~1.5 minutes and cascades through system to reduce occupancy.
  • Occupancy Management (ρ = +0.78): Primary constraint on discharge speed. Bed availability, not clinical capacity, limits throughput. Each 10% occupancy increase adds 10-15 minutes to ED stay.
  • Clinical Severity (ρ = +0.70): Strong predictor of admission pathway (Triage 1: 85% admitted, Triage 5: 5% admitted). Must risk-adjust performance targets by case mix.

All findings are statistically robust (p < 0.001) and hold when analyzing only non-flagged episodes, confirming genuine operational patterns.

Analysis Approach

Four-phase analytical framework:

  1. Phase 1: Descriptive Statistics (Day 2)
    • Univariate analysis: mean, median, standard deviation, skewness, kurtosis
    • Distribution visualizations: histograms, boxplots, temporal patterns
  2. Phase 2: Data Quality Assessment (Day 3)
    • Normality testing: Shapiro-Wilk, Kolmogorov-Smirnov, Anderson-Darling tests
    • Outlier detection: IQR method, flagged (not removed) for robustness
    • ~15% of episodes flagged as operational outliers (crises, complex cases)
  3. Phase 3: Bivariate Analysis (Day 4)
    • Spearman rank correlation (robust to non-normality, primary method)
    • Pearson correlation (comparison, assumes normality)
    • Significance testing: p-values and 95% confidence intervals
    • Sensitivity analysis: findings validated with/without outliers
  4. Phase 4: Clinical Interpretation
    • Causal chain validation (staffing → occupancy → wait time)
    • Operational implications for NHS management
    • Prioritized recommendations based on effect size and feasibility

Statistical Methods

Correlation Analysis: Spearman rank correlation (ρ) chosen over Pearson (r) because most variables show non-normal distributions (identified via Shapiro-Wilk, K-S, and Anderson-Darling tests). Spearman is robust to non-linearity and outliers.

Significance Testing: All p-values computed via two-tailed test with alpha = 0.05. Confidence intervals via Fisher's z-transformation.

Robustness Checks: Sensitivity analysis performed comparing full sample (n=2,000) with normal-only subset (n=1,700, excluding flagged outliers). All major findings remain significant and in same direction.

Key Findings

Finding 1: Staffing is the Primary Operational Lever

Correlation: ρ = -0.85, p < 0.001 (Highly Significant)

Evidence: Strong negative relationship between ED staff availability and time to first assessment. Each additional staff member reduces assessment wait by approximately 1.5 minutes.

Mechanism: More staff → Faster triage → Lower occupancy → Shorter downstream waits (cascade effect).

Implication: Staffing increases have direct, measurable impact on patient experience. Highest ROI intervention for wait time reduction.

Finding 2: Occupancy is the Primary Constraint on Discharge

Correlation: ρ = +0.78, p < 0.001 (Highly Significant)

Evidence: Strong positive relationship between ED occupancy percentage and time in ED. Each 10% occupancy increase adds 10-15 minutes to ED length of stay.

Mechanism: High occupancy → Limited available beds → Delays in admission/discharge → Bottleneck in ED → Longer waits.

Implication: Bed availability, not clinical capacity, is the limiting factor. Occupancy management (bed turnover) directly impacts ED performance.

Finding 3: Clinical Severity Drives Admission Pathways

Correlation: ρ = +0.70, p < 0.001 (Highly Significant)

Evidence: Linear relationship between triage category and admission rate:

  • Triage 1 (Immediate): 85% admitted
  • Triage 2 (Urgent): 55% admitted
  • Triage 3 (Soon): 35% admitted
  • Triage 4 (Standard): 15% admitted
  • Triage 5 (Minor): 5% admitted

Implication: Case mix varies clinically important. Must risk-adjust performance targets by severity. Cannot directly compare wait times between days with different case mixes.

Finding 4: Peak Hours Create Predictable Occupancy Surges

Pattern: Bimodal distribution with peaks at 10-14 and 18-22 hours

Evidence: Clear temporal pattern: peak hour occupancy 90-95%, off-peak (0-6 AM) occupancy 60-70%. 25-30 percentage point daily variation.

Mechanism: Morning peak (10-14): GP referrals, overnight admissions | Evening peak (18-22): End-of-day injuries, after-work presentations | Night quiet (0-6 AM): Minimal presentations, discharge opportunity.

Implication: Demand is predictable, not random. Enables proactive staffing deployment rather than reactive response.

Finding 5: Weekend Performance Gap is Staffing-Driven

Weekend wait times: +25 minutes vs weekdays

Evidence: Weekend waits average 270 minutes; weekday waits average 245 minutes. Gap exists despite similar case mix. Driven by ~15% lower weekend staffing.

Implication: Performance degradation is not clinical (severity similar) but operational (staffing mismatch). System designed for weekday demand, unprepared for weekend volume.

Finding 6: All Findings are Statistically Robust

Significance: All major correlations p < 0.001 | Robustness: Hold when excluding flagged outliers

Evidence: Sensitivity analysis comparing full sample (n=2,000) with normal-only episodes (n=1,700) shows all correlations remain significant and in same direction. Difference in effect size < 0.10 for all relationships.

Implication: Findings represent genuine operational patterns, not artifacts driven by extreme cases. Safe for stakeholder recommendations.

Limitations & Caveats

Important Methodological Notes

1. Synthetic Data: This analysis uses synthetic (simulated) data for portfolio demonstration. Real NHS data may show different patterns. Use this framework and methodology for real data analysis; don't operationalize these specific recommendations without validation.

2. Cross-Sectional Analysis: Analysis uses correlations from single-year snapshot. Temporal dynamics, causal lags, and intervention effects not modeled. Time-series analysis recommended for real data.

3. Confounding: Occupancy ↔ ED time relationship may be bidirectional (long ED times cause occupancy, or occupancy causes long waits). Cannot determine causation from correlation alone. Requires experimental or advanced modeling approach.

4. Unmeasured Variables: Analysis does not include bed turnover time, discharge planning quality, staff expertise, process efficiency, or equipment availability. These could confound observed relationships.

5. Missing Data: Length of stay in hospital bed (~70% missing for non-admitted patients) not used in primary analysis. Assumes missing completely at random.

6. Generalization: Findings specific to this dataset. Cannot generalize to other trusts/regions without validation. Staffing-occupancy relationship, for example, depends on ED layout, process flows, local policies.
