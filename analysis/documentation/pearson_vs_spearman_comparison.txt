
================================================================================
PEARSON vs SPEARMAN CORRELATION COMPARISON
================================================================================

WHY COMPARE BOTH METHODS?

On Day 3, we found most variables are NON-NORMAL:
  - time_in_ed_mins: RIGHT-SKEWED (skewness = 1.18)
  - ed_occupancy_pct: BIMODAL (skewness = 0.27)
  - hour_of_day: STRONGLY BIMODAL
  - ed_staff_available: DISCRETE (not continuous)

Pearson assumes normality → can be misleading for these
Spearman is robust to non-normality → more reliable

METHODOLOGY COMPARISON:

PEARSON CORRELATION (r):
  Assumption: Linear relationship with normal distributions
  Strength: Assumes data measured on interval scale
  Weakness: Sensitive to outliers and violations of normality
  Use when: Data are normally distributed and continuous

SPEARMAN CORRELATION (ρ):
  Method: Ranks data, then correlates ranks
  Strength: Robust to outliers, non-linearity, non-normality
  Weakness: Less powerful if data ARE truly normal
  Use when: Data are non-normal, ordinal, or have outliers

WHAT TO LOOK FOR IN COMPARISON:

1. AGREEMENT (difference < 0.10):
   → Both methods agree on relationship strength
   → Correlation is ROBUST
   → Trust either coefficient

2. DISAGREEMENT (difference > 0.15):
   → Methods disagree on strength
   → Usually means Pearson is inflated/deflated by non-normality
   → TRUST SPEARMAN (more robust for this data)

3. SIGN REVERSAL (r positive, ρ negative or vice versa):
   → Rare but signals complex non-linear relationship
   → Investigate further!

TOP CORRELATIONS COMPARISON:

Staffing ↔ Assessment Time:
  Pearson r = -0.84 | Spearman ρ = -0.85
  Difference = 0.01 → HIGH AGREEMENT, both robust

Occupancy ↔ ED Time:
  Pearson r = 0.68 | Spearman ρ = 0.78
  Difference = 0.10 → LOW AGREEMENT, Pearson underestimates
  (due to right-skewed ED time distribution)

Staffing ↔ Occupancy:
  Pearson r = -0.70 | Spearman ρ = -0.72
  Difference = 0.02 → HIGH AGREEMENT, both robust

Triage ↔ Admission:
  Pearson r = 0.69 | Spearman ρ = 0.70
  Difference = 0.01 → HIGH AGREEMENT, both robust

Hour ↔ Occupancy:
  Pearson r = 0.18 | Spearman ρ = 0.22
  Difference = 0.04 → HIGH AGREEMENT for weak correlations

SUMMARY STATISTICS:

Across all 15 correlations tested:
  - Mean difference: 0.06
  - Max difference: 0.10
  - High agreement (diff < 0.10): 14/15 (93%)
  - Low agreement (diff ≥ 0.10): 1/15 (7%)

Interpretation: Both methods mostly agree. Spearman slightly higher in most
cases, indicating Pearson slightly underestimates some relationships due to
non-normality. This validates our choice of Spearman as primary method.

FOR INTERVIEWS:

Q: "Why did you use Spearman instead of Pearson?"

A: "I performed normality testing on Day 3 and found that several key variables
(ED time, occupancy, hour of day) violated the normality assumption. Pearson
correlation assumes linear relationships with normally distributed data, so
it can be biased when these assumptions are violated. Spearman's rank-based
method is robust to non-normality, outliers, and non-linear monotonic
relationships, making it more appropriate for operational NHS data.

To validate this choice, I computed both Pearson and Spearman correlations
for all relationships. The methods showed high agreement (mean difference
0.06 across all pairs), confirming that the main findings are robust. Where
they did disagree, Spearman was typically slightly higher, indicating Pearson
was slightly underestimating relationships in the presence of skewed distributions."

KEY TAKEAWAY:

For non-normal NHS operational data, SPEARMAN is the more robust and
appropriate choice. Pearson comparison validates this choice by showing
high agreement on main findings.
