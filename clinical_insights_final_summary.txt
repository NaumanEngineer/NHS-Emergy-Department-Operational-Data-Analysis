KEY CLINICAL INSIGHTS FROM BIVARIATE ANALYSIS (DAY 4)

WHAT WE DISCOVERED:

✓ Staffing is the primary operational lever (ρ = -0.85)
  → Each staff member saves ~1.5 minutes assessment wait
  → Cascades through system to reduce occupancy and ED time
  → Highest ROI intervention for wait time reduction

✓ Occupancy is the primary constraint (ρ = +0.78)
  → Bed availability, not clinical capacity, limits discharge
  → Each 10% occupancy increase adds 10-15 min ED time
  → Discharge planning directly impacts ED performance

✓ Clinical severity is real and important (ρ = +0.70 with admission)
  → Case mix drives both ED time and admission rates
  → Triage 1: 85% admitted, Triage 5: 5% admitted
  → Must risk-adjust performance targets

✓ Temporal patterns are predictable (bimodal peaks 10-14, 18-22)
  → Peak hour occupancy 90-95%, off-peak 60-70%
  → Allows proactive staffing deployment
  → Weekend effect driven by staffing, not demand

✓ All findings are statistically robust (p < 0.001, hold without outliers)
  → Real relationships, not random noise
  → Confidence intervals narrow
  → Sensitive analysis confirms robustness

INTERVIEW TALKING POINTS (Band 7/8a Level):

Q: "What did your analysis reveal about ED performance drivers?"

A: "Through bivariate correlation analysis of 2,000 episodes, I identified
three interconnected drivers of ED performance. First, staffing emerges as
the primary operational lever (ρ = -0.85, p < 0.001), with each additional
staff member reducing assessment delays by approximately 1.5 minutes. This
cascades through the system via reduced occupancy.

Second, occupancy—not clinical complexity—is the primary constraint on
discharge speed (ρ = +0.78 with ED time, p < 0.001). This suggests bed
availability, not staff clinical capacity, limits throughput. Each 10%
occupancy increase adds 10-15 minutes to ED length of stay.

Third, clinical severity strongly predicts admission pathways (ρ = 0.70)
but explains only ~40% of ED time variation, indicating that occupancy
and staffing effects are independent of case mix.

I used Spearman correlations (robust to the non-normal distributions
identified in Day 3 testing) and confirmed robustness via sensitivity
analysis—all findings held when analyzing only non-flagged episodes,
excluding the 15% of outliers.

The implications are clear: operational improvements (staffing, occupancy
management) will have greater impact than clinical process changes alone."
